Analysis

AI did not develop in a straight line. Three forces drove every major breakthrough: big data, GPU hardware, and algorithmic advances. The 2012 AlexNet moment was the convergence of all three. Before it, two AI winters demonstrated that ambition without infrastructure produces stagnation. Cloud platforms like AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure then made large-scale training accessible without requiring supercomputers.

Key players — OpenAI, Google DeepMind, NVIDIA, Anthropic, Meta, and Microsoft — each contributed a different layer of the stack. The EU AI Act in 2024 marks the first time regulation has caught up, signaling that responsible AI is now both an ethical expectation and a legal one.

Key Insight

"AI progress depends not only on ideas but on funding, computing power, and realistic expectations. The AI winters were not failures of imagination — they were failures of infrastructure readiness. Every modern breakthrough traces back to the moment hardware finally caught up with the vision."

My Reflection

Building this timeline shifted how I think about the technology I work with every day. As a QA engineer on mainframe systems, I was already working with infrastructure built in an era before modern AI. Mapping the full arc from the Turing Test to ChatGPT helped me see that the tools emerging now are not replacements for rigorous engineering: they are extensions of it. What surprised me most was the AI winters. Periods where the ideas were right but the hardware and funding were not ready. That pattern resonates with QA experience too. Good design means nothing without the infrastructure to support it. This timeline gave me a clearer mental model of where I am starting my AI/ML journey and what it takes to build things that last.
